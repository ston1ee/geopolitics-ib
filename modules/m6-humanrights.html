Module 6: Human Rights & International Law

🛡️ Protecting Individual Dignity Globally

6.1 Evolution of Human Rights

Human rights are basic rights and freedoms that belong to every person in the world, from birth until death.

  • Magna Carta (1215): Early limits on absolute power.
  • Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948): The foundational document of the modern HR system.
  • International Covenants (1966): ICCPR (Civil/Political) and ICESCR (Economic/Social/Cultural).

Key Institutions

UN Human Rights Council

Inter-governmental body within the UN system responsible for strengthening the promotion and protection of human rights.

International Criminal Court (ICC)

Investigates and tries individuals charged with the gravest crimes: genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity.

6.2 Challenges to Human Rights

Debates often center on the tension between universalism and cultural relativism:

  • Universalism: Rights apply to all humans regardless of culture or nationality.
  • Cultural Relativism: Rights should be interpreted within the context of specific cultures and traditions.
  • State Sovereignty: States often claim that HR is an internal matter, resisting outside interference.

Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

A global political commitment which was endorsed by all member states of the United Nations at the 2005 World Summit in order to address its four key concerns to prevent genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing and crimes against humanity.
